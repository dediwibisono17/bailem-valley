In Brief This is an extraordinary festival and has become a visitor attraction in Papua. The Baliem Valley Festival was originally a tribal war event between the Dani, Lani, and Yali tribes as a symbol of fertility and prosperity. A festival that is an arena for inter-tribal power struggles and has been passed down for generations but of course it's safe for you to enjoy. 8-10/08/2023

The Baliem Valley Festival lasts for three days and is held every August to coincide with the month of the Indonesia's independence celebrations. Initially it was first held in 1989. What is special is that this festival begins with scenarios that trigger wars such as kidnapping residents, killing tribal children, or raiding newly opened fields. The existence of this trigger caused the other tribes to take revenge so the raid was carried out. This attraction does not make revenge or enmity a theme but instead has a positive meaning, namely “Yogotak Hubuluk Motog Hanorogo”-which means Hope for Tomorrow which Must Be Better than Today.

The tribes in the Papuan tribe, although experiencing modernization, still adhere to their customs and traditions. One of the most prominent is the men's clothing of the Dani tribe, which only covers their genitals or is called koteka. The koteka is made from dried water gourd skin and is equipped with a head covering made of bird of paradise or cassowary feathers, while the Dani women wear skirts made of grass or fern fiber called sali. When carrying pigs or sweet potato crops, the women carry them with a string bag or noken tied to their head. The Dani are used to fighting to defend their village or to avenge the tribe members who died. Anthropologists explain that the "Dani war" is more a display of prowess and extravagance in clothing and decorations than a war to kill the enemy. War for the Dani displays competence and enthusiasm rather than a desire to kill. The weapons used are 4.5 meter long spears, bows and arrows. Often, because of war people are injured rather than killed, and the wounded are quickly carried off the battlefield.

Today, the Dani war is held annually at the Baliem Hill Festival in Wamena during the month of August (see Calendar of Events). In this festival, the highlight of the event is the battle between the Dani, Yali, and Lani tribes when they send their best warriors to the battlefield wearing their best regalia. This festival is enlivened by a Pork Feast cooked underground accompanied by traditional Papuan music and dance. There are also handmade arts and crafts on display or for sale.

Each tribe has its own identity and one can see a clear difference between them according to their costumes and koteka. Dani men usually only wear a small koteka, while Lani men wear a larger one, because their bodies are larger than the average Dani men. Meanwhile, men of the Yali tribe wear a long and slender koteka tied by a rattan belt and tied at the waist.
